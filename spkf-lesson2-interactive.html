Step 1: How to Use PREFER

Language Step 1
Two correct structures — both mean exactly the same thing. Click each card to see examples.
Structure 1: prefer + verb-ing + to/rather than + verb-ing
I prefer cycling to taking the bus.
I prefer eating to cooking.
I prefer baking cakes at home rather than buying them in shops.
I prefer reading a newspaper rather than watching TV.
Structure 2: prefer + to verb + rather than + verb
I prefer to cycle rather than take the bus.
I prefer to eat rather than cook.
I prefer to bake cakes at home rather than buy them in shops.
I prefer to read a newspaper rather than watch TV.
Key Rule: Keep structures consistent. V-ing + V-ing OR to+verb + verb. NEVER mix them (e.g. prefer cycling rather than to walk = WRONG).

Extra Step: Discourse Markers

Extra Language
Use these discourse markers to sound more like a native speaker. Click any card to see the example.
actually / in fact
Introduces something surprising or that slightly changes what was said before.
It is actually a lot cheaper to go to that supermarket.
needless to say
Another way of saying obviously without sounding rude.
Needless to say, he will not get into university this year.
also / in addition
Used to add extra or further information.
In addition, it is better for the environment and it is also cheaper!
besides
At the start of a clause = as well as that / anyway.
Besides, it is cheap and I cannot afford a car.

Model Answer 1: Letters or Emails?

Sample Q&A
Do you prefer writing letters or sending emails?
I prefer sending emails rather than writing letters. They are more convenient and faster than traditional letters, so I can send something and get a reply quite quickly. I usually email my teachers when I have questions, and they often respond the same day.
Preference: prefer + V-ing + rather than + V-ing
Comparatives: more convenient / faster than
Linker: so
Example: daily habit

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Error Correction
Incorrect
"I prefer to cycling rather than to walking." (wrong: to + V-ing)
"I prefer reading rather than to watch TV." (mixed structures)
"Emails are more faster than letters." (double comparative)
"I prefer emails." (no comparison, no reason)
Correct
"I prefer cycling rather than walking." (V-ing + rather than + V-ing)
"I prefer to read rather than watch TV." (to + verb + rather than + verb)
"Emails are faster than letters." (one-syllable: add -er only)
"I prefer sending emails rather than writing letters because they are faster and more convenient."
Key Rules: (1) Keep structures consistent: V-ing + V-ing OR to+verb + verb. (2) Never use "more" with short adjectives (faster NOT more fast). (3) Always compare BOTH options and give a reason.

Discourse Markers: Deep Dive

Language Focus
Understand the exact meaning and usage of each discourse marker. Click any card to see a full example.
actually / in fact
Introduces something surprising or that slightly changes what was said before.
It is actually a lot cheaper to go to that supermarket.
needless to say
Another way of saying obviously without sounding rude or arrogant.
Needless to say, he will not get into university this year.
also / in addition
Used to add extra or further information. In addition is more formal than also.
In addition, it is better for the environment and it is also cheaper!
besides
At the beginning of a clause = anyway or as well as that. Adds an extra reason or point.
Besides, it is cheap and I cannot afford a car.

IELTS Speaking Tips for Part 1

IELTS Tips
Follow these tips to maximise your score in IELTS Speaking Part 1 preference questions.
DO
State your preference clearly first
Mention BOTH options from the question
Use at least one comparative adjective
Add a discourse marker (in addition, besides)
Give a specific personal example
Speak for 3-5 sentences per answer
DO NOT

Give one-word answers
