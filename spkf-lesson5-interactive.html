Step 1: The Present Perfect

Language Step 1
Use the Present Perfect to talk about changes that have happened up to now.
Structure: have/has + past participle
She has invested lots of money in a KTV bar.
The government has spent lots of money on public transport recently.
The traffic situation has become a lot worse in the last few years.
Restaurants and eateries have changed a great deal since I was young.
The local government has replaced them with tall apartment buildings.
Use "has" with:
he / she / it / singular noun
The city has changed. / It has improved.
Use "have" with:
I / you / we / they / plural noun
Shops have changed. / They have improved.
Key Time Expressions: in recent years / in the last five years / since I was young / over the past decade / these days / recently / nowadays

Step 2: Past vs Now Comparison

Language Step 2
Use past simple to describe the past and present simple to describe now. Click any card to see an example.
Past (was/were/used to)
My town was small; now it is quite big.
The streets used to be very quiet; now they are noisy.
The shops sold poor-quality clothes; now they sell designer fashions.
Before many people ate at small eateries, but now people choose expensive restaurants.
Now (is/are/now)
The streets are now much wider than before.
Now the local government has replaced them with tall apartment buildings.
Now a wide range of Vietnamese and international restaurants line every street.
These days, the city is much more modern and developed.
Key Contrast Linkers
but now ; now whereas now however at the same time

Step 3: Comparative Adjectives

Language Step 3
Use comparatives to explain how things have changed. Click any card to see an example.
1 Syllable: add -er
wide → wider
big → bigger
fast → faster
tall → taller
clean → cleaner
Ends in -y: change to -ier
busy → busier
pretty → prettier
noisy → noisier
tidy → tidier
friendly → friendlier
2+ Syllables: add more
beautiful → more beautiful
convenient → more convenient
elegant → more elegant
modern → more modern
expensive → more expensive
Key Structure: X + is/are + comparative + than + time in the past
My city is prettier than it was ten years ago.  /  The city is a lot greener than it was when I was a child.

Intensifiers with Comparatives

Language Step 3
Use these intensifiers to make your comparatives stronger and more natural. Click any card to see an example.
much + comparative
The streets are now much wider than before.
a lot + comparative
The city is a lot bigger than it was ten years ago.
far + comparative
These restaurants are far more expensive than the old eateries.
slightly + comparative
Life is slightly busier than it used to be.
considerably + comparative
The city is considerably more modern than it was before.
even + comparative
The roads are even wider than they were last year.
Strategy: Use "much" or "a lot" for big changes, "slightly" or "a bit" for small changes. This shows you can express degree precisely — a key IELTS skill!

Model Answer 1: Hometown Changes

Sample Q&A
How has your hometown changed in recent years?
Yes, it has changed quite a lot in recent years. It used to be quite small and quiet, but now it is much bigger and busier than before. The local government has built more roads and buildings, which makes the town more modern and better developed overall. You can see more shopping areas and public spaces now.
Present Perfect: has changed / has built
Past vs Now: used to be small → is bigger
Comparatives: bigger / busier / more modern
Natural feature: used to... / you can see...

Model Answer 2: Shops

Sample Q&A
How have shops changed in your city in the last five years?
Yes, they have changed quite a bit. Shops used to sell fairly basic and low-quality products, but now they offer more fashionable and better-quality goods. A lot of new supermarkets and malls have appeared, so shopping has become much more convenient than it used to be.
Present Perfect: have changed / have appeared / has become
Past vs Now: used to sell → now offer
Comparatives: more fashionable / better-quality / more convenient
Structure: than it used to be

Model Answer 3: Restaurants

Sample Q&A
How have restaurants in your country changed since you were young?
They have definitely changed a lot. When I was younger, most places were quite small and simple, whereas now they are cleaner and more modern than before. Many restaurants have become more expensive, but at the same time, the food and service tend to be better and more professional.
Present Perfect: have changed / have become
Past vs Now: were simple → are cleaner
Comparatives: cleaner / more modern / more expensive
Linkers: whereas / at the same time

Model Answer 4: Transportation

Sample Q&A
How has transportation changed in your city?
It has improved quite a lot. In the past, traffic used to be slower and less organised, but now it is faster and more convenient than before. The government has invested in better roads and public transport, so getting around the city is much easier these days.
Present Perfect: has improved / has invested
Past vs Now: used to be slower → now faster
Comparatives: faster / more convenient / much easier
Feature: these days

Model Answer 5: Lifestyle

Sample Q&A
How has your lifestyle changed in recent years?
Yes, it has changed quite a bit. I used to spend more time playing outside, but now I spend more time studying and using technology. My daily routine is busier than before, although it is also more productive, since I can use online tools to support my learning.
Present Perfect: has changed
Past vs Now: used to spend → now spend
Comparatives: busier / more productive
Linkers: although / since

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Error Correction
Incorrect
"The city change a lot." (missing has/have)
"Shops have changed. They are more good." (more good = WRONG)
"The city is more bigger than before." (double comparative)
"It changed." (no comparison, no description)
Correct
"The city has changed a lot." (has + past participle)
"Shops have changed. They are better now." (better = irregular comparative)
"The city is much bigger than before." (much + comparative)
"It used to be small, but now it is much bigger than before."
Irregular Comparatives: good → better | bad → worse | far → further/farther. Never say "more good" or "more bad"!

Irregular Comparatives

Grammar Reference
These adjectives have irregular comparative forms. Click any card to see an example.
good → better
The food is much better now than it used to be.
bad → worse
The traffic situation has become a lot worse in recent years.
far → further/farther
People can travel much further than they could before.
NEVER say these
more good → should be better
more bad → should be worse
gooder → should be better
badder → should be worse
Memory Tip: good/better/best | bad/worse/worst | far/further/furthest. These three are the most important irregular comparatives for IELTS Speaking.

IELTS Speaking Tips for Part 1

IELTS Tips
Follow these tips to maximise your score in IELTS Speaking Part 1 change questions.
DO
Use Present Perfect (has/have + past participle)
Compare past and present clearly
Use "used to" for past habits/states
Use comparative adjectives with intensifiers
Use "than before" / "than it used to be"
Use better/worse (not more good/more bad)
DO NOT
Forget to use has/have (say "it change")
Use "have" with singular (the city have)
Say "more good" or "more bad"
Use double comparatives (more bigger)
Forget to compare past and present
Give one-word answers
Remember the Formula!
Present Perfect + Past vs Now (used to be... but now...) + Comparatives (much bigger/more convenient than before)
